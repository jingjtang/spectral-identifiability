\section*{Introduction}
Public-health surveillance systems rely on downstream indicators such as confirmed cases, hospitalizations, and deaths to infer the trajectory of infection incidence\cite{irons2021estimating, jahja2022real, miller2022statistical}. However, these observables do not measure infection incidence directly. Instead, they arise only after infected individuals progress through a sequence of biological, behavioral, and reporting steps—symptom onset, testing or care-seeking, laboratory processing, and administrative reporting—each introducing delays, partial ascertainment, and additional sources of noise\cite{eales2024key, costa2021covid, greene2021nowcasting}. Consequently, surveillance data provide only an indirect and temporally distorted view of the underlying epidemic process. Because rapid changes are progressively attenuated before they appear in downstream observations, surveillance systems have a finite temporal resolution: sufficiently short-lived variations in transmission may no longer remain statistically distinguishable, limiting their identifiability regardless of the inference algorithm subsequently applied. This information-loss process is illustrated schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro}, where distinct upstream trajectories become nearly indistinguishable after delay filtering and observation noise.

A wide range of statistical and mechanistic approaches have been developed to reconstruct latent epidemic trajectories from delayed observations, including back-projection\cite{becker1991method}, deconvolution\cite{goldstein2009reconstructing}, Bayesian latent-incidence reconstruction\cite{chitwood2022reconstructing}, and renewal-based inference\cite{cori2013new, gostic2020practical}. Despite these methodological advances, reconstructing latent incidence from delayed observations remains an ill-posed inverse problem because multiple upstream trajectories may be consistent with the same downstream observations\cite{miller2022statistical}. Stable inference therefore requires regularization, prior distributions, or mechanistic constraints that select among multiple trajectories compatible with the observed data \cite{miller2022statistical, becker1991method, king2016statistical}. These assumptions appropriately quantify uncertainty within a specified model, but conditional uncertainty is not equivalent to observation-level identifiability: a narrow posterior does not imply that the observations uniquely determine the inferred trajectory. When the observations weakly constrain fine-scale temporal variation, different reconstruction assumptions can yield substantially different upstream dynamics while remaining equally consistent with the observed downstream data\cite{miller2022statistical,chitwood2022reconstructing}.

Related questions have been investigated within several complementary theoretical traditions. Within epidemiology, the primary question is how latent incidence should be reconstructed from delayed observations under specified epidemiological assumptions\cite{chitwood2022reconstructing, miller2022statistical}. In control theory and state-space modeling, observability characterizes whether hidden states are recoverable from noisy measurements under a specified dynamical system, while filtering provides algorithms for estimating those states.\cite{kalman1960new, ljung1998system}. In much of the classical observability literature, the central question is whether latent states can be reconstructed under specified dynamical and observation models. Statistical detection theory instead asks whether competing signals or hypotheses can be distinguished in the presence of noise and formulates likelihood-based measures of detectability that underpin ideal-observer analyses in communication systems, signal processing, and medical imaging\cite{green1966signal, barrett2013foundations, barrett1998objective}. Although these traditions respectively emphasize epidemic reconstruction, latent-state recoverability, and statistical distinguishability, none directly addresses the central question: given realistic delay distributions and observation noise, which temporal differences between candidate upstream epidemic trajectories remain statistically distinguishable in the downstream observations? Answering this question requires linking the epidemic observation process to a likelihood-based measure of statistical distinguishability, while explicitly incorporating the effects of epidemic delay and observation noise.

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro}, the delay distribution acts as a low-pass filter whose frequency response strongly suppresses rapid temporal variation, transforming the upstream signal before it reaches the data stream used for inference\cite{chitwood2022reconstructing, mcgough2020nowcasting}. In the frequency domain, convolution has a natural interpretation in which delay kernels act as strong low-pass filters that preserve slow, long-term trends while sharply attenuating rapid fluctuations \cite{oppenheim1999discrete, brigham1988fast}. Consequently, abrupt changes or high-frequency oscillations in the underlying epidemic process are smoothed out before appearing in surveillance data and may become statistically undetectable once observation noise is taken into account.

Here we develop a general spectral framework that quantifies the statistical distinguishability of candidate upstream epidemic trajectories under delayed and noisy observations. By combining the frequency response of epidemiologically realistic delay distributions with observation-model-specific noise, we derive a separability functional that determines whether differences between upstream trajectories remain recoverable in downstream surveillance data. The framework yields explicit identifiability limits across common observation models and characterizes which temporal features are fundamentally recoverable, independent of the particular reconstruction method employed.
