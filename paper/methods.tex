\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Spectral Representation for the Delay Model}
The observation process considered throughout this study is summarized schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro}A--C. Let \(U(t)\) denote the upstream process (e.g., latent infections) and \(D(t)\) the downstream observable (e.g., confirmed cases or deaths). Let \(g(\tau)\) be a nonnegative delay kernel supported on delays \(\tau \ge 0\) and normalized so that \(\int_0^\infty g(\tau)\, d\tau = 1\). A conversion rate $p\in(0,1]$ represents the probability that an upstream event generates a downstream observation. A common example is the ascertainment rate, which describes the proportion of latent infections ultimately recorded as confirmed cases. Because testing- or reporting-related changes typically occur on coarse temporal scales\cite{colman2023ascertainment, eales2023dynamics, ward2024real} relative to the support of $g$, we treat $p$ as locally constant.

The downstream process is modeled as
\begin{equation}
D(t)
= (U*h)(t) + \varepsilon(t),
\qquad
h(\tau)=p\,g(\tau),
\label{eq:delay-convolution}
\end{equation}
where \(*\) denotes convolution,
\[
(U*h)(t)=\int_{0}^{\infty}U(t-\tau)h(\tau)\,d\tau,
\]
and \(\varepsilon(t)\) is a zero-mean residual satisfying
\(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon(t)\mid U]=0\). Hence,
\[
\mathbb{E}[D(t)\mid U]=(U*h)(t).
\]

For additive Gaussian observation models, \(\varepsilon(t)\) corresponds directly to Gaussian noise. For count-based observation models such as Poisson or negative binomial sampling, this representation should instead be interpreted as a mean-plus-residual decomposition, where \(\varepsilon(t)\) denotes deviations from the conditional mean.

Because convolution in the time domain corresponds to multiplication in the frequency domain, it is natural to examine \eqref{eq:delay-convolution} spectrally. Taking Fourier transforms yields
\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}(f)
= \widehat{U}(f)\,\widehat{H}(f) + \widehat{\varepsilon}(f)
= p\,\widehat{U}(f)\,\widehat{G}(f) + \widehat{\varepsilon}(f),
\label{eq:fourier-delay}
\end{equation}
where \(\widehat{X}(f)\) denotes the Fourier transform of \(X(t)\). In particular, \(\widehat{H}(f) = p\,\widehat{G}(f)\). When \(p=1\), equation~\eqref{eq:fourier-delay} reduces to the delay-only setting without conversion loss. This frequency-domain representation highlights the attenuating effect of delay processes: for many epidemiological delay distributions, the magnitude \(|\widehat{G}(f)|\) decreases with frequency, thereby attenuating high-frequency upstream variation before it reaches the downstream process \(D(t)\).

This framework extends naturally to multilayer delay structures in which several delay mechanisms act sequentially, such as incubation, testing, reporting, and hospitalization delays. Let $g_1,g_2,\ldots,g_L$ denote normalized delay kernels associated with successive stages, and let $p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_L$ denote the corresponding conversion rates. The overall conversion rate is
$p_{\mathrm{tot}}=\prod_{\ell=1}^{L} p_\ell$, while the overall delay kernel is given by the convolution $g_{\mathrm{tot}}=g_1 * g_2 * \cdots * g_L$. The effective impulse response therefore becomes $h_{\mathrm{tot}}(\tau)=p_{\mathrm{tot}}\,g_{\mathrm{tot}}(\tau)$, and the downstream conditional mean satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[D(t)\mid U]=(U*h_{\mathrm{tot}})(t).
\]
In the frequency domain, the corresponding transfer function factorizes as

\begin{equation}
\widehat H_{\mathrm{tot}}(f)
=
\prod_{\ell=1}^{L}p_\ell
\prod_{\ell=1}^{L}\widehat G_\ell(f).
\end{equation}
Thus, sequential delay mechanisms compound their spectral attenuation multiplicatively, progressively suppressing high-frequency upstream variation before it reaches the downstream process.


\subsection*{Frequency-Domain Criterion for Upstream Identifiability}

We consider identifiability of the upstream process by asking whether two candidate trajectories \(U_0\) and \(U_1\) can be statistically distinguished based on the downstream observations. We pose the binary hypothesis test
\begin{equation}
H_0: U = U_0,
\qquad
H_1: U = U_1,
\end{equation}
From \eqref{eq:fourier-delay}, the conditional mean of the downstream spectrum under either hypothesis satisfies


\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\widehat{D}(f)\mid H_i]=p\,\widehat{G}(f)\,\widehat{U}_i(f), \quad i\in\{0,1\}
\end{equation}

Hence, the frequency-wise difference of the expected downstream signals is
\begin{equation}
\Delta\widehat{D}(f)
= p\,\widehat{G}(f)\,\bigl[\widehat{U}_0(f) - \widehat{U}_1(f)\bigr]
\end{equation}

To quantify the detectability of this contrast, we introduce
\(N_{\mathcal M}(f)>0\) as an observation-model-specific effective noise
scale, where \(\mathcal M\) denotes the observation model. Its precise
interpretation depends on the likelihood.

For additive stationary Gaussian noise,
\(N_{\mathcal M}(f)=S_\varepsilon(f)\) is the noise spectral density, and the
resulting weighted spectral distance corresponds directly to the Gaussian
likelihood geometry. For independent count observations, the variance depends
on the time-varying conditional mean and does not, in general, yield a
frequency-wise diagonal likelihood. In those settings, we use conservative
uniform variance bounds together with Parseval's identity to obtain a spectral
upper bound on the Kullback--Leibler divergence. Thus,
\(N_{\mathcal M}(f)\) should be interpreted generally as an effective
likelihood-based noise scale, rather than as a literal Fourier-domain variance
under every observation model.

As illustrated schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro}F, only the portion of upstream spectral contrast that survives delay filtering relative to the effective observation noise contributes to downstream distinguishability. We define the model-specific instantiation of the common separability
functional as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J_{\mathcal M}(U_0,U_1)
&=
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
\frac{|\Delta\widehat D(f)|^2}
     {N_{\mathcal M}(f)}
\,df
\\[4pt]
&=
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
\frac{|p\,\widehat G(f)|^2}
     {N_{\mathcal M}(f)}
\left|
\widehat U_1(f)-\widehat U_0(f)
\right|^2
\,df .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:J-def}
\end{equation}

Because \(U(t)\), \(g(t)\), and the downstream mean are real-valued,
their Fourier transforms are conjugate symmetric, and the integrand in
\eqref{eq:J-def} is even in frequency. Accordingly, numerical calculations
use the equivalent one-sided representation over nonnegative frequencies,
with the standard factor-of-two correction for interior positive-frequency
components; full discrete implementation details are provided in
Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_S1}.


When the observation model is clear from context, we write
\(J(U_0,U_1)\) for \(J_{\mathcal M}(U_0,U_1)\). In every case,
\[
J_{\mathcal M}(U_0,U_1)
=
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
w_{\mathcal M}(f)
\left|
\widehat U_1(f)-\widehat U_0(f)
\right|^2df,
\]
where
\[
w_{\mathcal M}(f)
=
\frac{|p\,\widehat G(f)|^2}{N_{\mathcal M}(f)}.
\]
The delay kernel therefore determines the common spectral geometry of the problem, whereas the observation model determines the scale against which
the surviving downstream contrast is evaluated.

For epidemiologically realistic delay distributions,
\(|\widehat G(f)|^2\) often decays rapidly with frequency. Unless the effective noise scale decreases sufficiently rapidly to offset this
attenuation, the spectral weight \(w_{\mathcal M}(f)\) becomes small at high frequencies. Consequently, differences concentrated at short temporal scales may contribute little to downstream separability even when their upstream amplitudes are substantial.


\subsection*{Detectability Under Different Observation Models}

In the hypothesis-testing framework introduced above, the effective noise scale \(N(f)\) depends on the observation model. We therefore instantiate the separability framework for three commonly used observation models: additive Gaussian noise, Poisson counting noise, and negative-binomial counting noise. Although these models induce different variance structures, they all lead to likelihood-based bounds relating the separability functional \(J(U_0,U_1)\) to statistical distinguishability. Full derivations are provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:Appendix_S3}.

For any decision rule \(\phi\), define the total testing error
\[
R(\phi)
=
P_0(\phi=H_1)
+
P_1(\phi=H_0),
\]
where \(P_0\) and \(P_1\) denote probabilities under the two hypotheses defined above. Small values of \(R(\phi)\) indicate that the two upstream trajectories can be reliably distinguished from downstream observations.

Likelihood-based arguments then imply that, for any target total testing error level \(\alpha\in(0,1)\), each observation model admits a corresponding non-identifiability threshold. When \(J(U_0,U_1)\) falls below this threshold, every statistical test satisfies
\[
\inf_{\phi} R(\phi)
\ge
\alpha,
\]
so \(U_0\) and \(U_1\) cannot be reliably distinguished from downstream observations alone.

Across all three observation models, the same qualitative mechanism emerges. Delay attenuation enters through \(|\widehat G(f)|^2\), conversion loss through \(p^2\), and observational uncertainty through the effective noise scale \(N(f)\). Statistical distinguishability is therefore governed by the same weighted spectral distance \(J(U_0,U_1)\), with observation models differing only in their variance structure and corresponding non-identifiability threshold.
